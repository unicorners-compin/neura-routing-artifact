## Current Quantitative Story

### 1. Shock Response

- In the calm interval before the hotspot, `NEURA` remains near `1.1 KB / 10 ms`.
- Over the same interval, `Triggered-TE` remains near `311 KB / 10 ms`.
- `OSPF-TE` remains near `378 KB / 10 ms`.
- `Bandit Routing` remains near `626 KB / 10 ms`.
- `TE+ECMP` is included in the delivery panel of the manuscript figure. It stays in the same high-service regime as the strongest baselines during the burst window.
- At shock onset, `NEURA` starts from a quiet background and rises only when local stress appears.
- `Triggered-TE` preserves stronger burst-window service than `NEURA`, but it does so from a much hotter control baseline.
- Over the same burst window, `NEURA` averages about `22` route changes per control epoch.
- `Triggered-TE` averages about `12` route changes per control epoch.
- `OSPF-TE` averages about `108` route changes per control epoch.
- `Bandit Routing` averages about `3779` route changes per control epoch.

### 2. Blast Radius

- `NEURA` keeps `100%` of hotspot-window control activity within one hop.
- Its update share is about `11.7%` at the hotspot and `88.3%` one hop away.
- `OSPF-TE` spreads only about `14.0%` of updates within one hop.
- The remaining `OSPF-TE` activity spreads to `2` hops and beyond.

### 3. Mitigation Cost Under Stress

At the representative `3x` hotspot workload:

- `NEURA`: `93.6%` burst delivery, `9.51 MB` control traffic
- `Triggered-TE`: `97.4%` burst delivery, `54.23 MB` control traffic
- `OSPF-TE`: `97.3%` burst delivery, `59.64 MB` control traffic
- `TE+ECMP`: `98.2%` burst delivery, `115.15 MB` control traffic
- `Bandit Routing`: `97.9%` burst delivery, `99.58 MB` control traffic
- `NEURA`: about `100` route changes per node
- `Triggered-TE`: about `118` route changes per node
- `OSPF-TE`: about `172` route changes per node
- `TE+ECMP`: about `242` route changes per node
- `Bandit Routing`: about `6381` route changes per node

At the harsher `5x` workload:

- `NEURA`: `85.8%` burst delivery, `10.57 MB` control traffic
- `Triggered-TE`: `87.2%` burst delivery, `56.73 MB` control traffic
- `OSPF-TE`: `86.9%` burst delivery, `59.64 MB` control traffic

This is the main operating-point result. `NEURA` does not lead on throughput, but it keeps the control budget far lower under stress.

### 4. Startup and Path Stretch

- `NEURA` startup convergence: `40 ms`
- `Triggered-TE` startup convergence: `40 ms`
- `OSPF-TE` startup convergence: `88 ms`
- `Bandit Routing` startup convergence: `62 ms`

Startup control budget before full reachability:

- `NEURA`: `1.76 MB`
- `Triggered-TE`: `3.36 MB`
- `OSPF-TE`: `2.73 MB`
- `Bandit Routing`: `3.22 MB`

At the representative `3x` hotspot workload, the manuscript now reports burst-window path stretch instead of another relief-time summary. This metric is used to show that the localized control law does not achieve low control overhead by allowing arbitrarily poor routes.

- `NEURA`: `1.048`
- `Triggered-TE`: `1.031`
- `OSPF-TE`: `1.007`
- `Bandit Routing`: `1.002`

This figure now answers two questions directly:

1. `NEURA` starts cheaply and converges early
2. its lower control overhead still stays within a bounded path-inflation regime under stress

### 5. Continuous Chaos

Across three repeated hotspot shocks:

- `NEURA`: `9.14 MB` total control traffic, about `100` route changes per node, `7.4` service-loss ticks
- `Triggered-TE`: `48.43 MB` total control traffic, about `122` route changes per node, `6.8` service-loss ticks
- `OSPF-TE`: `52.09 MB` total control traffic, about `210` route changes per node, `7.2` service-loss ticks
- `TE+ECMP`: `99.85 MB` total control traffic, about `282` route changes per node, `6.4` service-loss ticks
- `Bandit Routing`: `87.01 MB` total control traffic, about `5645` route changes per node, `4.0` service-loss ticks

This is the strongest repeated-disturbance result:

1. `NEURA` keeps the total control burden dramatically lower
2. `NEURA` also keeps cumulative route churn low
3. service remains useful under sustained disturbance
4. stronger baselines preserve slightly better service only by paying much higher cumulative control cost and churn

### 6. Safety Indicators

Post-startup safety indicators were added to separate routing-correctness effects from the control/service tradeoff.

Worst post-startup indicators over the representative `ER-100`, `5`-seed runs:

- `NEURA` hotspot / chaos worst reachability: `99.87%`, worst loop ratio: `0.13%`, worst blackhole ratio: `0.00%`
- `NEURA` gray-failure worst reachability: `99.94%`, worst loop ratio: `0.06%`, worst blackhole ratio: `0.00%`
- `Triggered-TE` hotspot / chaos worst reachability: `98.97%` / `97.64%`, worst loop ratio: `1.03%` / `2.36%`, worst blackhole ratio: `0.00%`
- `Triggered-TE` gray-failure worst reachability: `99.11%`, worst loop ratio: `0.89%`, worst blackhole ratio: `0.00%`
- `OSPF-TE` and `TE+ECMP` show slightly larger transient blackhole ratios, up to `0.08%`
- `Bandit Routing` stays below `0.05%` blackhole ratio in the same runs
- final pair reachability returns to `100%` for `NEURA`, `OSPF-TE`, `TE+ECMP`, and `Bandit`
- `Triggered-TE` remains slightly below full recovery and does not restore a complete route table in every seed

This closes an important paper gap: the main `NEURA` tradeoff is not coming from persistent loops or persistent blackholes. It comes from how each control law balances local stress response against control traffic and route churn. The `Triggered-TE` comparison also shows that plain thresholded event-driven engineering does not automatically inherit the same safety behavior.

Transient-duration analysis sharpens that conclusion:

- `NEURA` hotspot loop-active ticks average `14.6`, but only `0.2` ticks remain after the hotspot ends
- under continuous chaos and gray failure, `NEURA` post-stress loop and incomplete-route tails drop to `0.0` ticks on average
- `Triggered-TE` accumulates mean post-stress incomplete-route tails of `25.2`, `13.6`, and `9.8` ticks in hotspot, chaos, and gray-failure runs
- the worst `Triggered-TE` post-stress incomplete-route episodes last `89`, `34`, and `49` ticks in those same scenarios

This is the more precise safety reading used in the current paper draft: `NEURA` can show low-amplitude transient loop activity during stress, but it does not leave the same long recovery tail as the nearest-neighbor event-driven engineering baseline.

- `Triggered-TE`: `50.5%` burst delivery, `75.66 MB` control traffic, about `210` route changes per node
- `OSPF-TE`: `55.5%` burst delivery, `72.97 MB` control traffic, about `664` route changes per node

Under `RGG-100` continuous chaos:

This is the intended reading of the spatial validation: absolute service falls for every method on the stricter geometric topology, but `NEURA` still occupies the same low-control regime and retains a sharply more local blast radius.

### 8. Gray Failure Validation

The supplemental gray-failure validation keeps the topology unchanged and degrades the egress service rate of one transit node to `30%` of nominal capacity for `300 ms`.

- `NEURA`: `92.5%` impairment-window delivery, `20.3 KB` mean control traffic per control epoch, `6.39 MB` total control
- `Triggered-TE`: `96.4%` impairment-window delivery, `329.3 KB` mean control traffic per control epoch, `41.68 MB` total control
- `OSPF-TE`: `97.3%` impairment-window delivery, `378.3 KB` mean control traffic per control epoch, `44.53 MB` total control
- `Bandit Routing`: `97.1%` impairment-window delivery, `630.7 KB` mean control traffic per control epoch, `74.43 MB` total control

This validation matters because the impairment is not a hard link-down event. Queue buildup is the trigger. The result shows that `NEURA` still follows the same operating point under a silent local degradation, while `Triggered-TE` closes much of the service gap only by paying a control budget that remains close to `OSPF-TE`.

### 9. Mechanism Attribution

Memory-targeted rebound study:

### 12. ns-3 Replay Validation

The new `ns-3` evidence is intentionally narrow. It does not re-implement the full routing protocol stack inside `ns-3`. Instead, it exports per-link `data` and `control` traces from the closed-loop routing simulator and replays them over actual `ns-3` point-to-point links with UDP/IP headers and shared device queues. This makes the evidence useful as an external realism check for two review-sensitive questions:

1. whether the large control-byte gap survives once protocol headers are present
2. whether `NEURA` keeps the same low-control operating point when control and data traffic share the same packet queues

The current formal replay uses:

- `ER-24`, `seed 51`
- `hotspot` and `repeated-disturbance` workloads
- replay scaling `u = 2.0 Mbps per simulator load unit`
- `10 Mbps` link rate
- `20-packet` device queue

This setting is intentionally tighter than the default replay sanity check because it produces visible queue contention while keeping the replay small enough to run quickly inside Docker.

At the `hotspot` replay point:

- `NEURA`: `350.5 KB` control transmit, `99.70%` data delivery, `12.37 ms` mean data delay
- `Triggered-TE`: `1.63 MB` control transmit, `99.96%` data delivery, `3.73 ms` mean data delay
- `OSPF-TE`: `1.65 MB` control transmit, `99.81%` data delivery, `13.14 ms` mean data delay

Under `repeated disturbance`:

- `NEURA`: `350.5 KB` control transmit, `99.25%` data delivery, `14.63 ms` mean data delay
- `Triggered-TE`: `1.63 MB` control transmit, `99.89%` data delivery, `3.89 ms` mean data delay
- `OSPF-TE`: `1.65 MB` control transmit, `99.53%` data delivery, `20.50 ms` mean data delay

This is the intended reading of the `ns-3` check:

1. the control-byte advantage of `NEURA` survives once real packet headers are counted
2. under tighter shared queues, `NEURA` still occupies the low-control operating region rather than collapsing into baseline-level control overhead
3. the service tradeoff remains visible in a more concrete packet-level execution model, which is consistent with the main paper claim that `NEURA` is an operating-point method, not a throughput-maximizing one

### 13. ns-3 Online Queue-Signal Sanity Check

The second `ns-3` check stays intentionally small and answers a different review-sensitive question: can a real `DropTail` device queue provide a stable online pressure signal for route switching, or does packet-level jitter immediately push the control law into route thrash?

This check does **not** re-implement the full distributed routing protocol. Instead, it uses a five-node, two-path packet-level topology in which:

- a primary UDP flow can reach the destination through path `A` or `B`
- repeated background bursts saturate only path `A`
- the controller samples actual `DropTail` occupancy on the source-side path queues every `100 ms`
- the source route is rewritten online using three simplified control styles: `NEURA`, `Triggered-TE`, and `OSPF-TE`

The current formal run uses:

- `10 Mbps` links
- `20-packet` device queues
- one `4 Mbps` primary flow
- two `8 Mbps` background burst windows

Formal outputs are:

- `artifacts/ns3_queue_signal_sanity_rate10_q20_sample100_pri4_bg8/summary.csv`
- `artifacts/ns3_queue_signal_sanity_rate10_q20_sample100_pri4_bg8/neura_timeline.csv`
- `artifacts/ns3_queue_signal_sanity_rate10_q20_sample100_pri4_bg8/triggered_te_timeline.csv`
- `artifacts/ns3_queue_signal_sanity_rate10_q20_sample100_pri4_bg8/ospf_te_timeline.csv`
- `logs/ns3_queue_signal_sanity_rate10_q20.log`

The main takeaway is qualitative rather than throughput-driven:

- `NEURA` switches once from the congested default path to the alternate path and shows `0` post-stress tail switches
- `Triggered-TE` switches `4` times in total, including `2` post-burst returns to the default path
- `OSPF-TE` rewrites the source route `12` times as the periodic controller repeatedly revisits the stressed path during the burst windows

Primary-flow delivery remains `100%` for all three methods in this toy topology, so this check should not be read as a service leaderboard. Its value is narrower: under packet-level `DropTail` queues, a memory- and guard-based controller still produces materially less route thrash than plain triggered or periodic engineering rules.

At the current formal point (`10 Mbps`, `20-packet` queue, `100 ms` controller sample):

- `NEURA`: `8.54 Mbps` overall TCP goodput, `6.89 Mbps` burst-window goodput, `14` route changes, `7` tail switches, `399` TCP cwnd-drop events
- `Triggered-TE`: `8.79 Mbps` overall TCP goodput, `7.36 Mbps` burst-window goodput, `42` route changes, `22` tail switches, `454` TCP cwnd-drop events
- `OSPF-TE`: `8.48 Mbps` overall TCP goodput, `6.77 Mbps` burst-window goodput, `21` route changes, `9` tail switches, `319` TCP cwnd-drop events

This is the intended reading of the TCP check:

1. `Triggered-TE` can recover slightly stronger TCP goodput, but it does so with roughly `3x` the route rewrites of `NEURA` and materially more post-stress switching
2. `NEURA` does not maximize transport throughput, which is consistent with the paper's main claim
3. the stricter trigger bounding of `NEURA` still translates into a lower route-flap burden under a real closed-loop TCP sender, rather than collapsing into the same churn profile as the simpler engineering baseline

### 15. ns-3 Online ER Validation

The fourth `ns-3` check strengthens the packet-level story beyond trace replay and the five-node online sanity topology. It uses ER-24 topologies across five seeds, installs UDP primary flows, applies hotspot and repeated background burst windows, samples real source-side `DropTail` queue occupancy every `100 ms`, and rewrites source host routes online.

This check still does **not** implement a full distributed routing protocol stack. Its purpose is narrower: to test whether the route-flap and packetized-control ordering survives in a medium-scale packet-level online setting.

Formal outputs are:

- `artifacts/ns3_online_er_validation_n24_s5_f10_rate10_q20/summary.csv`
- `artifacts/ns3_online_er_validation_n24_s5_f10_rate10_q20/detail.csv`
- `artifacts/ns3_online_er_validation_n24_s5_f10_rate10_q20/README.md`
- `logs/ns3_online_er_validation_n24_s5_f10_rate10_q20.log`

At the current formal point (`ER-24`, five seeds, ten flows per run, `10 Mbps` links, `20-packet` queues):

- Hotspot: `NEURA` reaches `97.9%` delivery with `0.003 MB` ns-3 control transmission, `10.6` route changes, and `0.6` tail switches.
- Hotspot: `Triggered-TE` reaches `98.3%` delivery with `0.007 MB`, `21.6` route changes, and `11.6` tail switches.
- Hotspot: `OSPF-TE` reaches `99.9%` delivery with `0.885 MB`, `29.2` route changes, and `12.0` tail switches.
- Repeated: `NEURA` reaches `95.7%` delivery with `0.004 MB`, `12.0` route changes, and `1.4` tail switches.
- Repeated: `Triggered-TE` reaches `96.6%` delivery with `0.014 MB`, `43.2` route changes, and `23.2` tail switches.
- Repeated: `OSPF-TE` reaches `99.8%` delivery with `0.895 MB`, `60.8` route changes, and `25.2` tail switches.

This is the intended reading of the online ER check:

1. `OSPF-TE` remains the strongest service-preserving baseline in this packet-level slice, but with a much larger packetized control budget.
2. `Triggered-TE` closes part of the service gap but creates materially more route churn and post-stress tail switching than `NEURA`.
3. `NEURA` preserves the low-control, low-tail-switch operating point under online queue feedback on medium-scale ns-3 topologies.
